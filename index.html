The chop bar

One pot, one street corner, no complaints.

Ahumaakye sits right on the Danquah Circle roundabout, a few steps from the Police Hospital — the kind of spot you find by following your nose, not a map.

There's no seating rush and no closing time worry: the coal pot stays lit from just after midnight until the sun's fully up, which makes it the reliable stop after a night shift, a night out, or a night that just won't end.

Expect a queue of taxi drivers, off-duty officers, and students from the hostels nearby — the great equalizer of Accra's night crowd.

What makes it waakye
  • Rice and beans boiled together with dried millet leaves (ahaebankye) for that signature brown-red colour
  • Served with a spoon of shito, never skimped
  • Built by the plate — you choose every layer that goes on top
  • Eaten standing, sitting on a bench, or straight from the car window
